Fixed acidity

The non-volatile acids in wine, contributing to its tartness. To determine the fixed acidity of wine, you typically use a method called titration.

Volatile acidity

The volatile acids in wine, responsible for undesirable vinegar-like flavors. Gas chromatography is a more sophisticated technique that separates and quantifies individual volatile compounds present in the wine.

Citric acid

Naturally occurring acid in wine, influencing its freshness and tartness. Standardized sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution as the titrant, and an appropriate indicator.

Residual sugar

Remaining sugar in the wine after fermentation, affecting sweetness. It might be on the back of the bottle or methods like Chromatographic methods like high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) can also be used to measure residual sugar in wine.

Chlorides

Concentration of salts in the wine, influencing taste and mouthfeel. Can be determined a simple titration method using a silver nitrate (AgNO3) solution as the titrant.

Free sulfur dioxide

Presence of sulfur dioxide in its unbound form, acting as a preservative and antimicrobial agent. Determining free sulfur dioxide (SO2) in wine typically involves a method called the Ripper titration.

Total sulfur dioxide

Total amount of sulfur dioxide present in the wine, including both free and bound forms. The total SO2 content is commonly determined by the Ripper titration method for free SO2 and then by subtracting the free SO2 from the total SO2 content.

Density

Mass per unit volume of the wine, influenced by sugar and alcohol content. Use a hydrometer or a densitometer.

pH

Measurement of the wine's acidity levels.Determining the pH of wine involves using a pH meter or pH indicator strips

Sulphates

Presence of sulfur-containing compounds, affecting wine aroma and preservation. To determine this use Ripper titration.

Alcohol

Percentage of alcohol by volume in the wine, influencing its body and perceived warmth. It is mentioned on the back of the bottle

Wine's pH and it's Colour

Red wines with higher acidity are more likely to be a bright ruby color, as the lower pH gives them a red hue. Higher pH, less-acidic red wines can take on a blue or purple hue. Wines with lower acidity can also take on a brown color because they’re more prone to oxidation.

Wine Hue's

If you look at a red wine under natural lighting conditions and over a white background, you’ll get a pretty accurate impression of its hue. It might be difficult to see at first, but young red wines (under 5 years) range in hue from red, to violet, to blue. You can see this hue by looking towards the edge of the wine as it hits the glass.

What do they indicate?

Wines with more red colored hue have a lower pH (high acidity). Wines with a violet colored hue range from around 3.4–3.6 pH (on average). Wines with a more blueish tint (almost like magenta) are over 3.6 pH and possibly closer to 4 (low acidity).

Grape variety vs Wine?

The grape variety chosen for winemaking profoundly impacts the resulting wine's character. Each grape brings its own unique set of attributes, including aroma, flavor, acidity, and tannin levels. For instance, Cabernet Sauvignon is renowned for its bold blackcurrant and cedar notes, while Chardonnay exhibits crisp apple and citrus flavors. These inherent qualities, coupled with factors like terroir and winemaking techniques, collectively shape the wine's profile and distinguish it from others. Thus, the selection of grape variety serves as a crucial decision for winemakers, influencing every aspect of the final product's taste, texture, and aging potential.
